Phase 01
The Download
Day 1 — The client walks in
Before any creative thinking begins, the team must absorb the client's world completely. This is not a standard briefing call — it's a deep, unstructured conversation designed to understand the client's business, fears, ambitions, and the real problem beneath the stated problem. David Ogilvy called it "doing your homework." Jon Steel called it "listening until the silence tells you something." At Chi+Ko &Tay, the Download is sacred — no laptops, no decks, just humans in a room, listening.
MW
Mary Wells Lawrence
Leads
Runs the Download session. She makes the client feel heard, respected, and excited. She asks the questions that no one else dares to: "What are you truly afraid of?" "What does winning actually look like?" She captures the emotional stakes — not just the business objectives. Her warmth disarms. Her intelligence captures everything.
JS
Jon Steel
Active Support
Sits beside Wells Lawrence, listening with a planner's ear. He's not taking notes on what the client says — he's noting what they don't say. The pauses. The contradictions. The gap between the stated brief and the real problem. He'll use these gaps later to reframe the entire challenge.
DD
David Droga
Active Support
Present as CEO to signal that this client matters. He reads the room — assessing whether this is a client who will let Chi+Ko &Tay do great work. He's already evaluating: is this a partnership that will produce Cannes-worthy output? If not, he'll politely decline after the meeting. Chi+Ko &Tay doesn't take every client.
RS
Rory Sutherland
Consulted
Joins by invitation when the client's problem involves consumer behavior, pricing, or decision-making. He asks the counterintuitive question early: "What if the problem isn't what you think it is?" His presence signals to clients that Chi+Ko &Tay thinks differently from every other agency.
Phase Output

The Download Document — a raw, unfiltered capture of the client's world. Not a brief. Not a strategy. Just the truth as the client sees it: their business reality, competitive pressures, cultural context, internal politics, and the stated problem. Steel annotates it with his observations about what's missing. Wells Lawrence adds relationship notes — what motivates the client contacts personally, what makes them nervous, what will make them heroes internally.

Phase 02
The Immersion
Days 2–10 — Go deep before you go wide
The team disappears into the client's world. Steel conducts field research — talking to real consumers, not focus groups. Yakob scans the cultural landscape. Sutherland digs into behavioral patterns. Ogilvy's team analyzes data and competitive positioning. No one touches a pencil. No one opens Photoshop. This phase is about earning the right to have an opinion.
JS
Jon Steel
Leads
Conducts ethnographic-style consumer research. He goes where the consumers are — their homes, stores, commutes. He doesn't ask "What do you think of this brand?" He asks about their lives and waits for the brand to appear naturally. He's looking for the one insight that will unlock everything. He comes back with stories, not data points.
RY
Rosie Yakob
Leads — Cultural
Runs the Culture Radar™ analysis. What's happening in the world that connects to this brand's category? What tensions are brewing in society? What are people talking about, worrying about, celebrating? She maps the cultural territory — identifying the spaces where the brand can authentically insert itself into conversation. She delivers a Cultural Landscape brief.
RS
Rory Sutherland
Active Support
Analyzes the behavioral economics of the category. Where are the irrational decisions? What cognitive biases are at play? What would happen if we reframed the entire problem? He's looking for the counterintuitive unlock — the insight that makes creatives gasp and strategists rethink everything they assumed.
DO
David Ogilvy
Active Support
Reviews all quantitative data — market share, brand health tracking, sales figures, competitive spending, media landscape. He builds the evidence base that the strategy must answer to. He's the one who will later prove the work's effectiveness. His research rigor gives Steel's qualitative insights the commercial backbone they need.
EC
Eugene Cheong
Active Support
If the campaign will run across multiple markets, Cheong leads the cultural sensitivity review. He ensures the team understands how the insight, the tension, and the visual language will travel across Asia-Pacific, EMEA, and the Americas. He flags cultural landmines early — before they become expensive mistakes.
Phase Output

The Immersion Pack — a living document containing: Steel's consumer stories and insight candidates, Yakob's Cultural Landscape, Sutherland's behavioral framing, Ogilvy's competitive and commercial analysis, and Cheong's cross-market sensitivity notes. This pack is shared with the entire team and becomes the shared foundation for all thinking that follows. No one who didn't read it gets to have a creative opinion.

Phase 03
Finding the Tension
Days 10–14 — The strategic unlock
This is the most critical intellectual moment in the entire process. The strategy team distills all the research into one thing: a human or cultural tension. The gap between how the world is and how people wish it were. This tension becomes the foundation for the brand platform and the creative springboard. TBWA calls it "Disruption." Chi+Ko &Tay calls it the Tension Model™. It's the single sentence that makes everyone in the room lean forward and say: "That's it."
JS
Jon Steel
Leads
Synthesizes all research into 3–5 candidate tensions. Each one is a one-sentence articulation of a human truth that the brand can uniquely resolve. He pressure-tests each against: Is it true? Is it felt? Can the brand credibly own it? Is it fertile for creative work? He presents them to the core team in a working session — not a polished deck.
BB
Bill Bernbach
Active Support
Sits in the tension session as the creative filter. For each candidate tension, he asks: "Can I make an ad from this?" Not a strategy deck — an ad. If the tension doesn't spark an immediate creative instinct, it's too intellectual. Bernbach ensures the strategic thinking is always in service of the work, never separate from it.
RS
Rory Sutherland
Active Support
Challenges each tension from a behavioral perspective. "This tension sounds true, but will it change behavior?" He pushes the team beyond emotional resonance toward behavioral effectiveness. He might propose reframing the entire problem: "What if we don't solve the tension — what if we flip it?"
LC
Lee Clow
Consulted
Clow drops in to gut-check. He's been making brands for 40+ years. He can feel in his bones whether a tension is big enough to build a platform on — or just clever enough for one campaign. He's looking for the one that can run for a decade. "Think Different" wasn't a campaign — it was a belief system. That's the scale he demands.
Phase Output

The Tension — a single sentence, written on one page, that captures the human truth the campaign will be built on. Below it: a paragraph of context, the evidence base that supports it, and a provocative question that opens the door for creative teams. This single page is the most important document in the entire process.

⛔
Gate 01 — Droga Decision

David Droga personally reviews the Tension before it becomes a brief. His test: "Would I bet the agency's reputation on this idea?" If yes, it advances. If no, Steel goes back. This gate prevents mediocre strategic thinking from entering the creative process. It's the single biggest quality-control moment at Chi+Ko &Tay.

Phase 04
Writing The Brief
Days 14–16 — One page that changes everything
At Chi+Ko &Tay, the creative brief is one page. One. It contains the tension, the single-minded proposition, the target audience's emotional state, the desired behavioral change, the brand truth, and a creative springboard — a provocative thought designed to ignite, not instruct. The brief doesn't tell creatives what to make. It tells them where to dig.
JS
Jon Steel
Leads
Writes the brief. One page. Every word chosen with the care of a copywriter. The brief must inspire, not prescribe. It must leave space for creative interpretation while being sharp enough to prevent the work from drifting. Steel has written hundreds of these — the one for "got milk?" was famously elegant in its simplicity.
DO
David Ogilvy
Active Support
Reviews the brief for commercial rigor. Does the proposition connect to a measurable business outcome? Can we prove effectiveness afterward? He adds the "evidence brief" — a companion document with the data that will later form the Effie and Cannes Effectiveness entry. The creative team never sees this document. But it exists.
RY
Rosie Yakob
Active Support
Adds a "Cultural Context Addendum" to the brief — a visual and written snapshot of the cultural moment. What's in the zeitgeist right now? What visual codes are emerging? What conversations are people having? This addendum gives the creative team a cultural runway to land their ideas in the real world.
Phase Output

The Chi+Ko &Tay Creative Brief — one page. Contains: The Tension, the Single-Minded Proposition, the Target Audience (described as a human, not a demographic), the Desired Response ("After seeing this, I will feel/think/do…"), the Brand Truth, the Mandatories, and the Creative Springboard. Accompanied by Yakob's Cultural Addendum and Ogilvy's Evidence Brief (for internal use only).

Phase 05
The Incubation
Day 16 — The Brief Drop
Steel presents the brief to the full creative team. Not in a conference room with a projector. In a living room — with coffee, music, and space. The briefing is a performance. It's designed to make creatives feel the tension in their gut, not just understand it in their heads. After the briefing: silence. The creatives are sent away to think. No meetings for 48 hours. Just marinating.
JS
Jon Steel
Leads — The Performance
Presents the brief as a story, not a document. He makes the creatives feel what the consumer feels. He brings artifacts: photos from his field research, audio recordings, objects from the consumer's world. The best briefs aren't read — they're experienced. Steel is a master of this craft. By the time he finishes, the room is charged.
DW
Dan Wieden
Active Support
Sets the creative conditions. He reminds the team: "Walk in stupid. Forget everything you think you know. The brief is a door — not a destination." He ensures no one jumps to execution. The first 48 hours are for wandering, reading, sketching, talking to strangers, watching movies, walking the city. Ideas need space before they need structure.
BB
Bill Bernbach
Consulted
Bernbach addresses the creative team briefly after Steel finishes: "Remember — the trick isn't knowing what to say. What makes all the difference is the way you say it." Then he leaves. His presence is a benediction. His absence gives the team permission to find their own voice.
Phase Output

Nothing tangible. The output is a charged creative team with a clear tension in their gut, a sharp brief in their pocket, and 48 hours of protected thinking time. This is the most undervalued investment in advertising. Chi+Ko &Tay treats it as sacred.

Phase 06
The Concepting
Days 18–28 — Where magic is made
The creative teams (art director–copywriter pairs) generate ideas. Hundreds of them. Scrawled on napkins, walls, whiteboards, phone screens. The Creative Directors review daily. The ECDs review every 2–3 days. The CCO sees only the best. Ideas are killed fast. Survivors get stronger. By the end of this phase, 3–5 campaign territories emerge — each representing a different way to express the tension.
LC
Lee Clow
Leads — Creative Direction
Reviews all concepts as Global CCO. He's not looking for ads — he's looking for ideas big enough to become platforms. "Can this run for ten years? Can it work in film, print, social, retail, and experience? Is it a campaign or a belief system?" He pushes teams beyond their first good idea to find the great one hiding behind it.
MS
Marcello Serpa
Leads — Visual Concepting
Drives the visual exploration. For each territory, he creates mood boards, visual systems, and rough layout approaches. His mantra: "If the product isn't the hero, start over." He ensures every concept has a visual idea, not just a verbal one. He kills any idea that requires a headline to explain itself.
SH
Susan Hoffman
Leads — Copy & Narrative
Writes and directs the verbal expression of each territory. She finds the voice — is it funny, warm, provocative, quiet? She writes manifesto lines, headlines, scripts. She ensures the brand has a voice that sounds like a human being, not a corporation. She and Serpa work in constant dialogue — art and copy as equal partners, just as Bernbach intended.
HK
Helmut Krone
Active Support
Reviews every layout and design approach. He strips away anything that doesn't serve the idea. He challenges visual conventions: "Why does this look like an ad? Make it look like the truth." His presence keeps the design standard impossibly high. Nothing leaves the concepting phase that Krone hasn't blessed with his brutal, beautiful eye.
JS
Jon Steel
Consulted
Available on-call. When a creative team is stuck, they call Steel. He doesn't tell them what to make — he re-tells the consumer story from the Immersion, reframes the tension, or shares an anecdote from his research that unlocks a new angle. The best strategists are the best creative unlocks.
Phase Output

3–5 Campaign Territories — each presented as a "concept board" containing: the territory name, the core idea in one sentence, a sample headline or manifesto, a rough visual direction (mood board + layout), and a list of potential executions across media. Each territory must answer the brief but in a distinctly different way.

⛔
Gate 02 — The Creative Council

All 3–5 territories are presented to the Creative Council: Clow, Serpa, Hoffman, Krone, Cheong, and Bernbach. They grade each territory 1–5. Only Level 4 and 5 work advances. Level 3 gets sent back with specific direction to elevate. Level 1–2 is killed. This is the internal Cannes jury — if it doesn't win here, it won't win anywhere.

Phase 07
The Crucible
Days 28–32 — Pressure makes diamonds
The surviving territories (usually 2–3) are pressure-tested from every angle. Strategy stress-tests for insight integrity. Production assesses feasibility and budget. The behavioral scientist checks for persuasion logic. Cheong tests for cultural translation. This is where good ideas become bulletproof ideas. The Crucible is brutal, loving, and necessary.
DD
David Droga
Leads — Integration Chair
Chairs the Crucible session. He's the only person at Chi+Ko &Tay who can hold creative, strategy, production, and client perspective in equal balance. He asks the hard questions: "Will this make the client a hero? Will this win awards? Will this sell product? Can we actually produce this?" He makes the final call on which territories go to the client.
EH
Elaine Hinton
Active Support
Provides production feasibility. For each territory: estimated budget, production timeline, recommended directors/photographers, technical challenges, and production format recommendations. She doesn't kill ideas for being expensive — she finds ways to make ambitious ideas producible. But she's honest about what's impossible.
RS
Rory Sutherland
Active Support
Stress-tests the persuasion logic. "Will this actually change behavior — or just win creative awards?" He checks for behavioral coherence: does the ad create the right emotional frame? Does the call to action match the decision architecture? He adds behavioral nudges to the execution — small changes that dramatically improve effectiveness.
EC
Eugene Cheong
Active Support
Cross-market translation review. Will this concept travel? What needs to adapt for Asian markets? Middle Eastern markets? Latin America? He identifies which elements are universal and which need local versioning. He also pressure-tests the visual craft — his 250+ Cannes Lions mean his eye for quality is non-negotiable.
MW
Mary Wells Lawrence
Active Support
Runs a "client simulation." She plays the client. She anticipates their objections, their board's concerns, their CFO's questions. If the team can't answer Wells Lawrence's challenges, they can't answer the client's. This rehearsal hardens the team's resolve and sharpens their storytelling for the presentation.
⛔
Gate 03 — The Droga Lock

Droga selects the final 2 territories (occasionally 3) and "locks" them. Once locked, no further changes to the core idea are permitted. Only craft refinement. This prevents the death-by-a-thousand-cuts that happens when agencies keep revising ideas out of anxiety. The locked territories go into craft.

Phase 08
The Craft
Days 32–42 — Make it undeniable
The locked territories are now brought to presentation-ready fidelity. This is where Serpa's art direction, Krone's design rigor, Hoffman's copywriting, and Hinton's production expertise converge to create materials so beautifully crafted that the client feels the idea before they understand it. At Chi+Ko &Tay, we don't present rough concepts. We present the future — so vividly that the client can already see it in the world.
MS
Marcello Serpa
Leads — Visual Craft
Creates the final visual expressions: polished layouts, film storyboards with cinematic framing, brand identity mockups, OOH simulations, digital prototypes. Every pixel is intentional. Every color is deliberate. The visual presentation must be so beautiful that even if the client doesn't buy the idea, they'll remember how it looked.
SH
Susan Hoffman
Leads — Copy & Script Craft
Polishes all written and spoken expression: final headlines, body copy, film scripts, social copy, manifesto. Every word is weighed. She reads scripts aloud to test their rhythm. She brings in voice actors for key animatic recordings. The words must feel like they were always there — inevitable, not invented.
HK
Helmut Krone
Active Support
Final design review. He checks every layout for intellectual clarity and visual tension. Does the eye know where to go? Is the idea communicated instantly? Is there anything extraneous? "If it's not doing a job, it's doing damage." Krone's standard ensures the final work has the timeless quality of the VW "Think Small" ad.
EH
Elaine Hinton
Active Support
Produces the animatics, sizzle reels, and mock-ups that bring film and experiential concepts to life. She commissions rough-cut animatics from top directors. She builds physical mockups for packaging or retail ideas. Her production craft at this stage is what separates a good presentation from an unforgettable one.
Phase Output

The Presentation Deck — 2 fully crafted campaign territories, each containing: the strategic foundation (one slide), the campaign idea (one sentence), a hero execution (film storyboard or animatic + key visual), 3–5 extension executions across media, a brand identity system (if applicable), a production plan with timeline and budget, and an effectiveness measurement framework. Everything is designed, not described.

Phase 09
The Presentation
Day 42–44 — The Standing Ovation
The client presentation is a performance. Not a PowerPoint read-through. Chi+Ko &Tay presents in a purpose-built space — black walls, perfect acoustics, a single screen. The presenting team is curated for chemistry: the strategist who found the tension, the creative who brought it to life, and the leader who ties it all together. The room should feel like the opening night of something extraordinary.
DD
David Droga
Leads — Opens & Closes
Opens the presentation with the vision — why this brief matters, what's at stake, why this is the biggest opportunity the brand has ever had. He sets the emotional temperature of the room. He closes the presentation with a call to action: "This is the work. Let's go make history." His presence signals that Chi+Ko &Tay's very best people are in the room for this client.
JS
Jon Steel
Presents — The Tension
Presents the strategic foundation. He tells the consumer story. He reveals the tension. He makes the client feel the problem before the creative team solves it. His presentation style is conversational, not corporate. By the time he finishes, the client is nodding — they've just heard their customer described more accurately than their own research department has ever managed.
LC
Lee Clow
Presents — The Creative
Presents the creative work. He doesn't describe ads — he unveils a vision of the brand's future. He walks the client through the hero execution, then expands outward: "Here's how this lives in social. Here's the retail experience. Here's the PR moment. Here's the cultural conversation you're starting." The work sells itself. Clow just gives it a voice.
MW
Mary Wells Lawrence
Presents — The Business Case
Presents the production plan, timeline, and investment framework. She translates creative ambition into business language. She answers the CFO's questions before they're asked. She makes the client feel that this isn't just a great campaign — it's a great business decision. She closes the commercial conversation with grace and confidence.
RS
Rory Sutherland
Available — The Closer
On standby for the Q&A. If the client pushes back on behavioral logic, pricing strategy, or asks "But will this actually work?" — Sutherland enters with a perfectly calibrated anecdote about human irrationality that reframes their concern as an opportunity. He's the secret weapon for skeptical CMOs and analytically-minded boards.
🎯
Gate 04 — Client Decision

The client selects a territory (or requests refinements). Wells Lawrence manages post-presentation communication with surgical care — addressing concerns without diluting the work. If the client asks for changes that would fundamentally compromise the idea, Droga personally intervenes. Chi+Ko &Tay protects the work. Always.

Phase 10
The Aftermath
Post-Presentation — The work after the work
At most agencies, the process ends at presentation. At Chi+Ko &Tay, it's just the beginning. The Aftermath ensures the approved work is produced at the highest quality, measured for effectiveness, and submitted to the world's most prestigious award shows. This is where Cannes Lions are actually won — not in the concepting phase, but in the relentless pursuit of craft and measurement that follows.
EH
Elaine Hinton
Leads — Production
Takes the approved concept into full production. Commissions directors, builds production timelines, manages budgets, oversees post-production. She brings Serpa and Hoffman into every production decision to ensure the finished work matches — or exceeds — the craft level of the presentation materials.
DO
David Ogilvy
Active Support
Sets up the effectiveness measurement framework from day one of production. Tracking brand lift, sales impact, media value, social sentiment — everything needed for Effie and Cannes Creative Effectiveness submissions. The measurement starts BEFORE the campaign launches, not after.
JC
Jay Chiat
Active Support
Begins the awards engine. He identifies the campaign's award potential, develops the submission strategy (which categories, which shows, which markets), and commissions the case study production team. Chiat treats awards with the same relentless energy he brings to new business — because at Chi+Ko &Tay, awards ARE new business.
DW
Dan Wieden
Active Support
Leads the post-mortem. What worked? What didn't? What would we do differently? His post-mortems are legendary for their honesty — no politics, no blame, just truth. Every post-mortem insight is fed back into the process for the next brief. This is how Chi+Ko &Tay gets better with every campaign, not just bigger.
Phase Output

The finished campaign in-market. Plus: an effectiveness tracking dashboard, award submission materials (case study films, entry decks, results documentation), a post-mortem report, and updated process learnings for the Chi+Ko &Tay Operating System. The cycle is complete — and the next brief is already waiting.
